## What Materials Do You Need to Get Started?

### Essential Supplies Breakdown

A productive workshop starts with the right materials. Here’s a basic list of what you’ll need:

- **Work Bench**: You can build a simple workbench using **2x4 lumber** — I spent about $30 on this.
- **Storage Solutions**: Shelving units can range from $40 for basic models to around $150 for more complex installations. I settled for **plastic shelving** from Walmart at $25, which was just right for my needs.
- **Lighting**: A good LED shop light costs about $20. I found an affordable model at Home Depot that brightened the entire shed efficiently without consuming too much power.
- **Tool Storage**: Simple pegboards are around $15 each. I bought two to keep my frequently used tools organized.
- **Working Tools**: Invest in a decent drill, screwdriver, and a few hand tools if you don’t have them already. I repurposed old tools I had stashed away, saving about $50.

### Finding Budget-Friendly Alternatives

By exploring thrift stores and garage sales, I was able to find additional furniture and tools at a fraction of the retail cost. For example, I snagged a sturdy stool for $5 and found several used tools, saving anywhere from 30% to 50% off retail prices.

## What's the Difference Between New and Used Tools?

Many DIYers ask about whether to go for new or used tools while renovating. I once made the mistake of splurging $150 on new tools, thinking they would be the best option. However, used tools can be just as effective if sourced wisely. 

- **Cost-Effective**: Used tools are often discounted 30% to 70% compared to new ones.
- **Performance**: Many seasoned builders prefer older, robust models that can often outlast newer versions — check sites like eBay where I found plenty of quality options at lower prices.

To sum it up, unless you need a specific feature or guarantee of longevity, used tools can often meet your needs and keep your budget intact.

## How Does Lighting Affect Your Workshop?

### Importance of Proper Lighting

Lighting might seem like a minor detail, but it greatly impacts productivity. Insufficient light can lead to poor craftsmanship and safety risks. I learned this when I initially tried to work under basic outdoor lights — and it didn’t work! 

- **LED Shop Lights**: Efficient yet bright. A good fixture originally costs around $20 and can last for years.
- **Task Lighting**: For detailed work, adding smaller task lights can help. I invested another $10 for a focused light on my workbench area.

#### Choosing the Right Bulbs

Don’t overlook the color temperature of bulbs; daylight white bulbs help in seeing true colors and details, critical for woodworking and precise tasks. I replaced an old fluorescent light with an LED daylight bulb, making a noticeable difference in my workspace's vibe and safety.

## Practical Security Tips for Your Shed Workshop

### Keeping Your Tools Safe

As I got deeper into my workshop project, I realized security was a significant concern. A shed made into a functional workspace could attract unwanted attention, and I had to ensure my tools and equipment stayed safe. 

- **Locks and Bolts**: Investing in a sturdy **padlock** cost me around **$10**. After experiencing a minor theft scare in my neighborhood, this was money well spent. I also reinforced the doors with some **extra screws** I had around, which took minimal effort but added crucial security.

### Alarm Systems

If you have the budget, consider setting up a basic security system. Though it wasn’t feasible for my $200 budget, many companies like **Wyze** offer affordable cameras starting at **$20**. Knowing I had the option gave me peace of mind.
