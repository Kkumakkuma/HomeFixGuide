That constant drip-drip-drip from your faucet is more than just annoying. A leaky faucet can waste over 3,000 gallons of water per year, adding unnecessary cost to your utility bill. The good news? Fixing a leaky faucet is one of the easiest plumbing repairs you can tackle yourself.

In this guide, we'll walk you through how to fix a leaky faucet step by step. No plumber required. Most homeowners can complete this repair in under 30 minutes with basic tools.

## What Causes a Leaky Faucet?

Before you grab your wrench, it helps to understand what's going on inside your faucet. The most common causes of a dripping faucet include:

- **Worn-out O-rings** - These small rubber rings create a watertight seal. Over time, they degrade and allow water to seep through.
- **Corroded valve seat** - The valve seat connects the faucet to the spout. Mineral buildup can cause corrosion and leaks.
- **Damaged cartridge** - In cartridge-style faucets, the cartridge controls water flow. A worn cartridge leads to drips.
- **Worn washers** - Compression faucets use rubber washers that wear out with repeated use.

Identifying your faucet type is the first step toward a successful repair. The four main types are compression, ball, cartridge, and ceramic disc faucets.

## Tools and Materials You'll Need

Gather these items before you start. Having everything ready makes the job go much smoother.

- Adjustable wrench
- Phillips and flat-head screwdrivers
- Replacement O-rings or cartridge (match your faucet brand)
- Plumber's grease
- Allen wrench set
- Clean cloth or towel
- Small container for parts

**Estimated cost:** $5 to $20 for replacement parts. **Time required:** 15 to 30 minutes.

## Step 1: Turn Off the Water Supply

This is the most important step, and the one people sometimes forget. Look under the sink for the shut-off valves. Turn them clockwise until they stop.

Once the valves are closed, turn on the faucet to release any remaining water pressure. This prevents an unexpected spray when you start disassembling things.

If you can't find individual shut-off valves under the sink, you may need to turn off the main water supply to your home. It's usually located near the water meter.

## Step 2: Remove the Faucet Handle

Most faucet handles are held in place by a screw, which is often hidden under a decorative cap. Use a flat-head screwdriver to gently pry off the cap.

Once exposed, use the appropriate screwdriver or Allen wrench to remove the handle screw. Pull the handle straight up and off the faucet body.

**Pro tip:** Place a towel in the sink drain to prevent small parts from falling down the pipe. You'll thank yourself later.

## Step 3: Inspect and Replace the Damaged Parts

Now you can see the internal mechanism. What you do next depends on your faucet type.

**For compression faucets:** Remove the packing nut with your wrench. Pull out the stem and inspect the rubber washer at the bottom. If it looks flat, cracked, or worn, replace it. Also check the O-ring on the stem.

**For cartridge faucets:** Pull out the cartridge (you may need a cartridge puller tool for stubborn ones). Take it to your local hardware store to find an exact replacement. Installing the wrong cartridge is a common mistake.

**For ball faucets:** These have multiple small parts including springs, seats, and O-rings. A ball faucet repair kit from the hardware store is your best bet. It includes all the parts you need for about $10 to $15.

**For ceramic disc faucets:** Remove the ceramic disc cylinder and inspect the neoprene seals. Clean any mineral deposits with white vinegar. Replace the seals if they show wear.

Apply a thin layer of plumber's grease to all O-rings and rubber seals before reinstalling. This helps create a better seal and makes future repairs easier.

## Step 4: Reassemble the Faucet

Put everything back together in reverse order. Take your time here. Rushing the reassembly is where most DIY mistakes happen.

Make sure all parts are seated properly and tighten connections snugly, but don't overtighten. Over-tightening can crack the faucet body or strip threads, creating a bigger problem than you started with.

Replace the handle and secure the screw. Pop the decorative cap back in place.

## Step 5: Test Your Repair

Slowly turn the water supply back on. We say slowly because turning it on full blast can cause water hammer, which puts stress on your pipes and connections.

Let the water run for a minute and check for leaks around the handle and spout. Turn the faucet on and off several times. Check under the sink for any drips at the supply connections.

If the drip is gone, congratulations. You just saved yourself $150 to $300 in plumber fees.

## When to Call a Professional

While most leaky faucets are straightforward repairs, there are times when calling a plumber makes sense:

- **The faucet body is cracked** - No amount of washer replacement will fix a cracked faucet. You'll need a full replacement.
- **Corroded pipes** - If you notice significant corrosion on the pipes beneath the sink, a professional should assess the situation.
- **The leak persists after repair** - If your faucet still drips after replacing the internal components, there may be a deeper issue with the valve seat or faucet body.
- **You're not comfortable with the repair** - There's no shame in calling a pro. A botched DIY repair can cause water damage that costs far more than a plumber's visit.

## Preventing Future Leaks

A little maintenance goes a long way in preventing faucet problems.

- **Don't overtighten handles** - Turning the faucet off with excessive force wears out washers and seals faster.
- **Clean aerators regularly** - Remove and clean the aerator screen every few months to prevent mineral buildup.
- **Address drips early** - A small drip today becomes a bigger problem tomorrow. Fix it as soon as you notice it.
- **Install a water softener** - If you have hard water, a water softener reduces mineral deposits that corrode faucet components.

## Save Money by Doing It Yourself

Fixing a leaky faucet is one of those home repairs that feels intimidating until you actually do it. The repair itself is simple, the parts are cheap, and the satisfaction of stopping that annoying drip is well worth the effort.

Most homeowners spend less than $20 and under 30 minutes to fix a leaky faucet. Compare that to a plumber's service call, which typically runs $150 to $300 for the same repair.

Start with this project, and you'll build the confidence to tackle bigger plumbing repairs down the road. Your wallet and your water bill will thank you.
