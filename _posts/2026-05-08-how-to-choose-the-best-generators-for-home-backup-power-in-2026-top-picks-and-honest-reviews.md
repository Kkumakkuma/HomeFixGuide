## What's the Difference Between Inverter and Conventional Generators?

When choosing a generator, the distinction between inverter and conventional models is crucial. 

- **Inverter Generators:** These generate cleaner power, making them safer for sensitive electronics. They're typically quieter and more fuel-efficient, although generally more expensive. 

- **Conventional Generators:** More powerful but less efficient, they produce a raw power output and can be noisy, which could be a concern in residential areas. However, they’re usually cheaper.

## How Does a Generator Work?

Generators convert mechanical energy into electrical energy through an electromechanical process. A fuel source—such as gasoline, propane, or natural gas—powers an engine that turns a rotor in a magnetic field. This motion generates electricity, which powers your home appliances during outages.

### Fuel Types: What Works Best?

1. **Gasoline:** Commonly found, user-friendly, and affordable, but has a shorter shelf life.
  
2. **Propane:** Burns cleaner and can be stored indefinitely, although availability can be a concern.
  
3. **Dual-Fuel:** Offers flexibility by allowing users to switch between gasoline and propane. This feature can be a lifesaver when one fuel type is unavailable.

## Practical Tips for Choosing the Right Size Generator

When selecting a generator, determining the right size is critical. In my research, I discovered that many consumers overlook this aspect, which can lead to inadequate power supply during an outage. 

### Calculate Your Power Needs

To accurately determine the generator size, start by calculating the wattage required for essential appliances during an outage. For instance, typical appliances you might want to power:

- **Refrigerator:** 600-800 watts
- **Sump Pump:** 1,000 watts
- **Lights (10):** 100 watts (10 x 10 watts)
- **Heating System or HVAC:** 1,500 watts

Adding these together, you might need around **3,200 watts** to cover basic needs. I found using an online wattage calculator incredibly helpful; I utilized one provided by Generac to ensure I didn’t miss any crucial loads.

### Rule of Thumb

A good rule of thumb is to opt for a generator that can handle **20-25%** more than your calculated needs. This buffer allows for start-up surges of appliances. So, if your calculated total is 3,200 watts, aim for a generator with at least 4,000 to 4,500 watts capacity.

## Personal Experience: The Impact of Maintenance on Generator Performance

Another critical lesson I learned from my generator research is that maintenance can significantly influence performance and lifespan. After I purchased my **Westinghouse WGen9500** for $999, I became diligent about upkeep, and it paid off.

### The Importance of Routine Maintenance

1. **Oil Changes:** Regular oil changes can help extend the life of your generator. I established a habit of changing my WGen9500’s oil every 100 hours of use, roughly corresponding to seasonal usage. I use the **SAE 30** oil recommended by Westinghouse, keeping a spare gallon on hand in my garage.

2. **Fuel Stabilizer:** After a summer camping trip, I discovered that the fuel in my generator’s tank had gone stale. Now, I always add a fuel stabilizer like **STA-BIL** before I store it for extended periods. This action has prevented starting issues when I need it most.

3. **Battery Care:** If you have a standby generator with a battery (like the Generac Guardian), make it a point to check its charge regularly. During a recent storm, my fate was almost sealed when I realized my backup battery was dead because I neglected to check it after my last use.
